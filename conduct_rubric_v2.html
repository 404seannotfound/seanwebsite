How to use the picture-book rubric

A version of the conduct rubric where every score level is a Sergio panel. Pick the picture that most closely matches what actually happened. The math is the same; the choosing is a little less abstract.
  1. Check the automatic escalators first. They override the scored total. One flag → floor of Elevated. Two or more → Severe.
  2. For each of the 10 factors, pick the panel that fits. Click a card to score. Click it again to clear. When two panels both feel close, pick the lower one and write a note explaining why.
  3. Deliberate. The score is a floor — never a ceiling. The committee can adjust up. The rubric never adjusts you down.

About this edition

  • Same math, more art. Section 1, the 0–5 grid, severity floors, and tier thresholds are identical to v1. Hash-links from v1 still work here.
  • The pictures aren't the rubric. The text under each panel is the rubric. The art is to make it easier to recognize what you're looking at.
  • Slow down. A picture is a stronger commitment than a checkbox. That's the feature.

§1 Automatic escalators

Eight conditions that bypass the scoring grid entirely. Once any of these are present, the conversation changes from "how bad" to "how fast."
Rules. Any one checked → minimum response: Elevated. Two or more → minimum: Severe. These floors override the Section 2 total.

§2 Pick a picture, score the factor

Each factor below is a panel of six cartoons — score 0 through score 5. Click the card that best fits what actually happened (not what could have happened). Use the notes field to record disagreement, caveats, or evidence.
Severity floors. Any single factor at 5 → minimum Elevated. A single factor at 4 → minimum Moderate. Two or more at 4+ → minimum Elevated. Floors stack with Section 1.
